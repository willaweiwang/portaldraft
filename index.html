Designing Without Platforms: A Protocol Engine Framework for Distributed Cultural Co-Creation


Abstract

This paper introduces the Protocol Engine, a design framework for facilitating distributed collaboration in traditional culture commons without relying on centralized digital platforms or formal governance structures. As global pressures challenge community-led cultural practices, many existing preservation models depend on institutional authority or commercial systems that may undermine local autonomy.

Through a longitudinal design-as-research engagement (2012–2023) focused on Dong polyphonic song traditions in rural China, this study examines how over 400 participants—including villagers, children, returning youth, and urban volunteers—co-created sustained cultural and educational outputs. These included illustrated songbooks, performance documentation, and co-designed tourism services.

Instead of top-down rules or contracts, the collaboration was enabled by emotionally grounded coordination mechanisms. The Protocol Engine emerged through iterative field practice and is articulated here as five interdependent design principles:

Atomic role modularity for plug-and-play participation,

Emotional anchoring through shared cultural identity,

Asynchronous coordination accommodating temporal diversity,

Lightweight, self-imposed protocols that require no enforcement,

Permeable cultural boundaries balancing inclusion with authenticity.

The research applies narrative reconstruction and axial coding to trace how design artifacts and informal rules co-evolved over time. Findings contribute to design theory by extending modular design thinking from technical systems to community-centered social infrastructures. The Protocol Engine offers design researchers and practitioners a replicable coordination model for cultural co-creation in non-market, low-infrastructure, and emotionally intensive contexts.

keywords:

Distributed Co-Design, Protocol-Based Collaboration, Cultural Commons, Modular Governance, Design-as-Research, Emotional Interdependency, Post-Platform Design

1. Introduction

As distributed collaboration increasingly becomes the norm in both professional and community design contexts, questions of coordination, role management, and shared authorship have become central to design research. Much of the existing literature assumes that such coordination is mediated through platforms—digital infrastructures that centralize communication, impose structure, and facilitate exchange. Yet many co-creation contexts operate without platforms, particularly in non-commercial, low-resource, or culturally embedded environments. These settings require alternative coordination models—ones that are lightweight, emotionally resonant, and flexible across temporal, social, and geographic boundaries.

This paper addresses this gap by introducing the concept of the Protocol Engine—a design framework for enabling distributed co-creation in cultural commons, without relying on centralized digital platforms or formal contracts. The concept arises from a decade-long design-as-research engagement (2012–2023) with Dong ethnic communities in Guizhou, China. The project supported co-creation efforts around Dong Grand Song, a traditional polyphonic vocal practice. Over 400 participants—including rural elders, children, returned youth, urban designers, musicians, and educators—collaborated to co-create illustrated songbooks, music videos, tourism experiences, and educational materials.

A critical challenge encountered throughout the project was the absence of formal governance mechanisms. Participants had varying levels of technical skill, cultural literacy, and availability. Many had never met before or even during collaborating. There were no contractual agreements, platforms, or financial incentives. Yet despite this, the project sustained momentum across a full decade. What made this possible?

The author argues that emotionally anchored coordination protocols emerged over time to guide participation, mediate accountability, and uphold cultural integrity. One defining moment occurred when a rural girl, nominated to attend an international cultural award ceremony, refused the opportunity, stating: “This has nothing to do with foreigners. They don’t understand our songs.” Her words captured the subtle but powerful boundaries of cultural authorship—boundaries not encoded in rules, but felt and defended through emotional commitment.

Drawing on reflective practice, narrative reconstruction, and axial coding, the author identifies and articulates a framework of five interrelated design principles that constitute what we call the Protocol Engine. These include modular role design, emotional anchoring, asynchronous coordination, soft protocols without enforcement, and permeable cultural boundaries. Together, they offer a coordination model particularly suited to low-infrastructure, high-identity contexts.

By foregrounding design without platforms, this paper contributes to emerging discussions in participatory design, design governance, and social infrastructure. It extends modular design thinking beyond technical artifacts to culturally embedded collaboration systems, and illustrates how emotion—not hierarchy—can serve as a viable basis for organizing distributed co-creation.

2. Literature Review

This study contributes to ongoing debates about how design can enable distributed, culturally embedded co-creation in contexts lacking formal infrastructure or governance. We structure the review into three interrelated domains: (1) distributed collaboration without platforms, (2) protocols as emergent governance in participatory design, and (3) modular and affective coordination in community-based design. Each section addresses a key coordination challenge that the Protocol Engine seeks to resolve.


2.1 Designing Distributed Collaboration Without Platforms

Much of the research on distributed collaboration in design presumes the existence of shared infrastructures—such as digital platforms, organizational charters, or predefined roles—to support coordination across distance and difference (Cash, Dekoninck, & Ahmed, 2017). These tools serve to mediate communication, define interdependencies, and enforce accountability. However, in many grassroots or culturally embedded design contexts, such infrastructure is absent or undesirable.

Light and Akama (2014) challenge infrastructure-centric assumptions by introducing the concept of “designing with care,” which emphasizes relational trust, emotional investment, and context-sensitive negotiation in design practice, demonstrating how trust and emotional investment generate adaptive coordination structures without relying on rigid platforms. Similarly, Björgvinsson, Ehn, and Hillgren (2012) argue that participatory design must contend with contested, emergent publics where stable roles or tools may not exist. In their “agonistic PD” approach, participation is not about consensus but about continuous negotiation across asymmetries.

This study builds on these insights by exploring a design context where 400+ participants—rural songmasters, children, returning youth, and urban designers—collaborated without a common toolset or authority. The Protocol Engine emerges as a coordination structure born not from platforms, but from accumulated interactions, shared meaning, and evolving emotional alignment.

Key design problem: How can coordination emerge in culturally rooted, low-infrastructure settings where platforms are unavailable or inappropriate?


2.2 Protocols as Emergent Governance in Participatory Design

Participatory design has evolved from a method of user inclusion to a theory of socio-technical world-making. Central to this evolution is the recognition that design does not merely produce artifacts, but also establishes conditions for collaboration—including governance, legitimacy, and continuity (Ehn, 2008; Binder et al., 2011; Simonsen & Robertson,2013).

The concept of infrastructuring (Le Dantec & DiSalvo, 2013) reframes participatory work as the long-term cultivation of frameworks, roles, and informal rules that persist beyond individual projects. Instead of delivering fixed outcomes, designers build incomplete structures that others can inhabit, adapt, and extend. Binder and colleagues (2015) describe this as governance-by-design, in which rules emerge not from contracts or leadership, but through shared practices and reflexive negotiation.

In the context of this study, formal agreements were rare, and no contract dictated participation. Instead, coordination stabilized around culturally resonant norms—respect for song elders, shared pride in Dong heritage, and informal accountability in peer groups. These “emotional protocols” governed who could do what, how tasks were distributed, and how disagreement was managed.

Key design problem: In the absence of centralized authority, how can co-creation be governed in culturally meaningful and adaptive ways?


2.3 Modular and Affective Coordination in Design

While design theory often emphasizes modularity as a strategy for managing technical complexity (Baldwin & Clark, 2000), its application to social systems of collaboration remains underdeveloped. In community-led or voluntary projects, participants may have shifting availability, partial skills, and uneven commitment. Designing for such instability requires modularity not only in task structure, but also in emotional labor and social participation.

Recent work in design for social innovation (Manzini, 2015) has begun to explore how loosely coupled networks can generate resilient collaboration through shared values and distributed initiative. Similarly, Foster and Iaione (2016) describe “urban commons” models where modular participation allows citizens to contribute at varying levels of intensity and expertise, enabled by a framework of shared rules.

This study extends such approaches by introducing the idea of atomic role modularity—a design principle wherein tasks are broken into plug-and-play components that tolerate fluctuation. More critically, it argues that emotional anchoring is not an obstacle to coordination but a prerequisite: when participants care, they commit—even in the absence of contracts. The Protocol Engine integrates both modular design and emotional interdependence to create a distributed system capable of long-term coherence.

Key design problem: How can collaborative systems be designed to absorb fluctuating commitment while maintaining emotional investment and cultural legitimacy?


2.4 Toward an Integrated Framework

Together, these three domains form the conceptual foundation for the Protocol Engine proposed in this paper. Distributed collaboration literature foregrounds the infrastructural void; participatory design offers a lens for informal governance and infrastructuring; and modular-affective theories suggest design principles that address human variability in commitment and emotion. The Protocol Engine draws from each of these to articulate a framework where protocols are not imposed, but emerge through participation, modularity, and cultural affect.


3. Research Context and Methodology

3.1 Research Site: Co-Creating Across Boundaries

This study is grounded in a decade-long design engagement (2012–2023) with Dong ethnic communities in Guizhou Province, Southwest China. The Dong Grand Song, an oral polyphonic vocal tradition listed by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity, serves as the focal cultural practice. While valued for its aesthetic and historical depth, Dong song transmission has become fragile due to school migration, economic outflow, and generational gaps. In this context, the researcher initiated a series of co-creation projects not as a facilitator of preservation, but as a designer embedded in evolving collaboration processes.

Over ten years, 215 participants—ranging from village elders, songmasters, and children to returning youth, urban designers, musicians, educators, and tourists—contributed in various capacities. Importantly, no centralized digital platform or contract-based governance mediated their participation. Instead, collaboration unfolded through modular tasks, informal agreements, and affective motivation. The fieldwork sites included two rural villages and multiple urban engagement points, linked by informal communication channels (e.g., QQ groups, WeChat, in-person gatherings). The study thus explores design in low-infrastructure, high-identity contexts.


3.2 Methodological Approach: Design-as-Research in Cultural Commons

The study adopts a Design-as-Research (DaR) framework (Jonas, 2007; Gaver, 2012), wherein design is not only a vehicle of intervention but a mode of knowledge generation. Rather than isolating variables or comparing fixed models, the researcher engaged iteratively through cycles of immersion, reflection, and collaborative prototyping. The central unit of inquiry was not a singular object, but the emergent co-creation ecosystem—its rules, roles, and relational patterns.

Key characteristics of this approach include:

Longitudinal embeddedness: Over a decade of situated engagement with local communities;

Reflective intervention: Projects were co-designed with stakeholders, and modified based on emergent needs and dynamics;

Epistemic participation: The researcher participated not only as observer or facilitator, but also as media maker, organizer, educator, and protocol co-architect;

Open-ended outcome horizon: Design outputs (e.g., illustrated songbooks, tourism service models, teaching tools) served as both artifacts and probes into collaborative dynamics.

This approach aligns with infrastructuring in participatory design (Ehn, 2008; Björgvinsson et al., 2012), where the designer seeds ongoing capability-building, rather than delivering final solutions. The focus of inquiry thus shifts from product to relational governance and collaborative capacity.


3.3 Narrative Reconstruction and Coding Strategy

To trace the emergence of protocols over time, the study employed a two-stage qualitative analysis rooted in narrative reconstruction and grounded theory coding (Charmaz, 2006; Corbin & Strauss, 2015). The researcher drew from a wide range of ethnographic material including:

Audio-visual documentation of rehearsals, performances, and workshops;

Interview transcripts with children, elders, and youth of the local communities and designers, educators, musicians, etc. who joined the project (2012–2023);

Design diaries, project notes, and field reflections;

Feedback logs from co-creators, funders, and external observers;

Digital traces from collaborative platforms (e.g., WeChat threads, QQ singing sessions).

In the first stage, narratives were constructed chronologically to capture the situated logic of participation—why individuals joined, how they interpreted their roles, and what tensions or moments of resonance they experienced. Particular attention was paid to emotional cues (pride, shame, belonging, resistance) as indicators of soft coordination structures.

In the second stage, narratives were subjected to open coding, focusing on recurring interaction patterns, relational framings, and decision points. These codes were grouped into axial categories that described the emergence of protocol functions: boundary-making, role adaptation, affective alignment, asynchronous timing, and modular substitution.

This analytical approach enables the study to articulate the Protocol Engine not as a static model, but as a pattern that emerged from lived practices—embodied, negotiated, and emotionally charged.


3.4 Research Ethics and Positionality

Given the long-term, embedded nature of the project, issues of positionality and ethical reflexivity were critical. The researcher’s roles—as designer, co-creator, videographer, facilitator, and fund-raiser—were not fixed, but evolved through dialogue with the community. Informed consent was obtained for all documentation and co-creation activities, and data were returned to communities in the form of educational tools, archives, and published materials.

Rather than extractive observation, the research pursued what Akama & Light (2015) call relational ethics—centering trust, reciprocity, and respect for cultural intelligibility. This ethical stance also shaped the core design logic of the Protocol Engine: protocols emerged not through enforcement, but through emotional legitimacy and mutual care.

3.5 Data Collection Strategy

Data were collected iteratively across four phases of field engagement (2012–2023), each involving different modes of participation and documentation. The following types of primary material formed the basis for analysis:

Video and Audio Documentation: Including rehearsal footage, singing sessions, co-creation workshops, and interviews across all phases.

Narrative Interviews: Semi-structured interviews with children, songmasters, returned youth, and urban collaborators.

Design Field Notes: The researcher's reflective journals, observational logs, and ideation sketches.

Digital Communication Records: Chat transcripts from QQ, WeChat, and co-design planning threads.

Artifacts and Prototypes: Co-created outputs including illustrated songbooks, tourist services, performance videos, and teaching tools.

Data were catalogued using a phased matrix aligned with the evolving goals of the project. All materials were coded chronologically, thematically, and relationally. Due to the longitudinal nature of the research, multiple return visits allowed for iterative feedback, validation, and contextual re-interpretation of previously collected data.


3.6 Analytical Framework

Analysis followed a grounded theory-inspired, narrative-centric process, incorporating three overlapping methods:

Narrative Reconstruction: Raw data were synthesized into first-person or collective narratives centered around specific individuals (e.g., a songmaster, a child, a designer). Each narrative emphasized situational motivation, tension, negotiation, and emotional affect. This step made visible the relational logic underpinning participation (Neimeyer, 2006; Bamberg, 2006; Rosenthal, 1993; Riessman,2008).

Open and Axial Coding: Drawing from Charmaz (2006), narrative units were coded line-by-line to identify key behavioral patterns (e.g., role fluidity, refusal to represent, asynchronous contribution). These were then clustered into axial categories (e.g., soft boundaries, modular governance, emotional activation).

Design Layer Mapping: Based on the coding, a systems map was created to trace how roles, timing, tools, and emotional factors interlocked over time to form what would later be defined as the Protocol Engine. This approach reflects the idea that mapping relational and temporal dynamics can support systemic understanding and design synthesis (Sanders & Stappers, 2008; Junginger, 2015; Jones, 2014).


3.7 Framework Development and Validation
The Protocol Engine framework was not predefined, but emerged iteratively across multiple project phases. Initial versions were abstracted from field patterns, then gradually formalized through:
– Design Prototypes: Partial toolkits and training templates were tested in the field (e.g., modular team roles, co-creation scripts, service blueprints), following a constructive design research ethos (Koskinen et al., 2011; Sanders & Stappers, 2014).
– Reflexive Practice: Researcher diaries and group debriefings captured shifts in participation behavior and perception over time, aligning with reflective evaluation in research-through-design (Zimmerman et al., 2007).
– Cross-Actor Validation: Informal feedback was solicited from diverse actors—village youth, teachers, designers, songmasters—on how they understood the “unwritten rules” of participation, echoing Binder et al.’s (2011) emphasis on performativity in socially embedded frameworks.
– Outcome Traceability: The final framework was validated against sustained outputs (e.g., project continuity, cultural ownership, lack of coordination breakdowns) across more than 10 years.

This cyclic method ensured that the framework was not merely descriptive but performative—it proved its coherence through practice (Binder et al., 2011).


3.8 Methodological Rigor and Trustworthiness
Several strategies were employed to ensure the trustworthiness of the research process (Guba & Lincoln, 1985):

– Triangulation: Multiple data types (narrative, video, digital communication) and stakeholder perspectives were cross-compared to increase credibility (Denzin, 1978).
– Longitudinal Consistency: The researcher’s continuous engagement across time and context allowed for dynamic verification of evolving patterns, reinforcing context-dependent knowledge (Flyvbjerg, 2006).
– Member Checking: Emerging concepts (e.g., protocol roles, emotional legitimacy) were refined through informal feedback loops and translated into actionable tools, resonating with participatory validation practices (Lincoln & Guba, 1985).
– Thick Description: Rich narratives capturing emotional, behavioral, and relational dimensions offered interpretive depth, following Geertz’s (1973) call for contextual immersion.
– Design Reflexivity: The research foregrounded how the designer’s presence and actions shaped the system, aligning with reflexive design practice where the researcher becomes part of the data ecology (Kimbell, 2012; Iversen et al., 2012).

Rather than aspiring to universal generalizability, this study emphasizes transferability—providing thick, transparent pattern articulations that allow practitioners in similar contexts to evaluate and adapt the framework (Stake, 1995; Guba & Lincoln, 1985).

Chapter 4: From Practice to Protocol – Designing Distributed Collaboration in a Cultural Commons

Design is often evaluated through its outcomes—products, systems, services. But in contexts where infrastructure is minimal, institutions are absent, and participants are fluid, design must instead focus on enabling conditions—for participation, trust, and meaning to emerge. This chapter traces the evolution of such conditions in a ten-year longitudinal design engagement with Dong ethnic communities in rural Guizhou, China. Centered on the preservation and revitalization of Dong Grand Song, the project began as documentary support for a cultural initiative and gradually evolved into a distributed, protocol-based co-creation system.

Through four phases—ethnographic immersion, decentralized co-design, modular creative production, and post-pandemic resilience—the chapter documents how governance, motivation, and collaboration emerged without contracts or platforms. Instead, what formed was a Protocol Engine: a set of informal, culturally grounded, and emotionally anchored mechanisms that enabled over 205 participants to contribute asynchronously, sustainably, and meaningfully across geographic, generational, and social boundaries.

This chapter is not merely descriptive. Each phase is analyzed through a design lens: What problem was being solved? What coordination mechanism emerged? What role did the designer play in enabling distributed agency? Through this narrative, the chapter provides the empirical foundation for the theoretical analysis that follows.

4.1 From Observation to Commitment (2012–2013)

*Design challenge: How can participation begin before structure exists?*


The project began not with a design directive, but with quiet observation. In 2012, the researcher entered Dimen Dong Village in Guizhou, China, as a volunteer with the Dimen Eco-Museum—an experimental community-based institution supporting local heritage preservation. At the time, the museum was funding a weekly Dong song class, hiring Geshi (master singers recognised by the local communities) to teach local children traditional polyphonic songs. Classes were held in the museum’s wooden hall, where children stood in rows, repeating each phrase sung by the Geshi. The transmission method was entirely oral and embodied—no lyrics, notation, or props were used.

As a visiting designer and documentarian, the researcher’s initial role was to observe, film, and support. However, the limits of this arrangement quickly surfaced. Children often began the session with enthusiasm, but their focus faded after 15–20 minutes. The repetitive structure—call and response without variation—proved cognitively and emotionally taxing. Older students who had developed fluency were visibly more confident and expressive; they also tended to be more extroverted and enjoyed greater visibility in school performances and village events. In contrast, children who struggled with the form or had less exposure tended to withdraw, especially in the presence of visitors.



Geshi teaching the Dong children how to singing dong song (插图和标注格式），Photo by the author


The learning environment was precarious. Most children would leave the village by Grade 5 to attend boarding schools in township centers, interrupting their exposure to oral traditions. The Geshi were paid small stipends, but lacked structured curricula, tools, or pedagogical training. No documentation or adaptive strategies existed for children with different learning styles. Yet beneath this fragility lay a clear emotional core: children who excelled at singing often expressed strong ethnic pride and a desire to share their heritage—though not necessarily with outsiders.



Dong children singing Dong songs in traditional costumes，2012，Photo by the author

A pivotal moment occurred when the Dimen children’s choir received the 2012 National Arts and Humanities Youth Program Award from the U.S. President’s Committee on the Arts and Humanities. A delegation was invited to Washington D.C. to attend the ceremony at the White House. While the event was framed as international recognition of intangible heritage, several top-performing students declined to go.“I don’t want to go abroad,” one said. “They won’t understand our songs.”Their reluctance was not due to fear or disinterest, but stemmed from a misalignment of recognition systems. In the children’s view, the recognicians from the Geshi, school and the community were way more meaningful than from a Country they were not heard of. While media coverage framed this as a cultural achievement, the internal ambivalence marked something deeper: Cultural worth was not measured abroad, but judged within the local inter-village system of song performance, where authority was earned through skill, lineage, and community visibility.



The author's interviews with Dong ethnic children and the Dong ethnic MV video recorded by the author，2012


In particular, the annual Spring Festival “duige” (对歌) competitions—where teams from surrounding Dong villages engage in multi-night song battles—were seen as far more consequential. In these events, one group sings a lyrical challenge, and the opponent must reply with a matching or better stanza in both lyric and rhythm. The losing side withdraws, and the contest continues until one village remains. Lasting up to seven consecutive days and nights, these intense rituals not only test musical ability but establish prestige hierarchies among villages with the Dong ethnic group. For the children who emerged in these cultural norms, success in this arena—recognized by peers, elders, and rivals—carried far more weight than a foreign honor.


duige are usually held in the Drum Tower, which is the most important public space for the Dong people. Honors won from past singing duets are hung on the walls,2015,Photo by the author


This distinction reveals a fundamental tension at the heart of cultural commons: who holds the authority to judge mastery and value? And what motivates individuals to pursue that mastery? In this context, children did not learn Dong songs for school credit or personal gain. Instead, their learning was propelled by emotional attachment, social recognition, and the desire to belong. Once they gained song competence, they were celebrated by teachers, featured in public events, and treated with admiration. This, in turn, strengthened their commitment and desire to “live up to” cultural expectations.

Rather than rushing toward innovation, this phase demanded a shift in the designer’s role: from observer to ethnographic listener, from documentarian to situated reflector. The key lesson was that protocols already existed—but in social memory, affective dynamics, and unspoken practices, not in formal policy or platforms. These informal structures would later inspire the Protocol Engine's core design logic.


In this phase, the designer’s role evolved from an observer to an ethnographic listener who could recognize the latent protocols embedded in cultural practices.




4.2 Small-Scale Co-Creation and Cultural-Economic Integration (2014–2017)

Design challenge: How can a distributed system emerge without central infrastructure?

After the initial phase of observation and participation, the researcher shifted to Miaolan Village—a more remote Dong community located in Congjiang County, Guizhou. Compared to Dimen, Miaolan had no institutional support such as a museum or external NGO, but it had a strong base of informal cultural actors: active songmasters, elder performers, youth with a desire to learn, and village leaders interested in community development. The researcher’s intent was no longer documentation, but to experiment with co-creation as a tool for distributed cultural resilience.

Two intersecting issues defined the entry point:

Cultural discontinuity: A youth song group funded by a one-time donation (200,000 RMB) had dissolved as many young people left to work in cities. Without consistent income or incentives, the practice of singing could not be sustained.

Economic precarity: Song performance, weaving, and craftsmanship existed but generated little income. The local economy was primarily agricultural, with few diversified revenue streams.

Instead of proposing a formal intervention, the researcher initiated a low-barrier co-creation framework. This model was not introduced as a fixed plan, but rather evolved through design-as-invitation: open calls, informal gatherings, role-play sessions, and collaborative experimentation. Participants included:

Urban designers who volunteered time to help with packaging design (e.g., local rice, wine, herbs), tourism brochures, and video materials;

Local elders and youth, who contributed stories, customs, and performance practices;

Visitors, students, and professionals, who took part in short-term co-creation camps, helping prototype tourism experiences such as homestays, weaving lessons, and song performances.

These collaborations yielded more than isolated outputs—they began to formalize protocols for engagement, including:

Co-created pricing menus for cultural experiences (e.g., 10 RMB per traditional performance per person; 50 RMB per night homestay including dinner);

Shared hosting responsibilities, distributed among participating families;

Story-based training materials created by and for villagers, on how to introduce their home, perform songs, or lead simple craft activities.


Villagers of all ages and genders voluntarily participate in the reception work, perform traditional music to welcome tourists, and can earn income in return, 2015, Photo by the author

To prevent elite capture (i.e., the concentration of tourist benefits among a few well-connected villagers), a local cooperative model was introduced. This was not a registered business entity, but a design artifact—a simple spreadsheet, hand-written contracts, and a jointly maintained visitor record book. The goal was social accountability through design visibility: everyone knew who received which guests, at what price, and who still needed to participate.

The designer's role evolved again—from embedded listener to system weaver, facilitating interactions that allowed protocols to emerge from use. There was no contract, no leadership hierarchy, and no centralized platform. What governed participation was:

Reciprocity and peer observation;

Emotional bonds formed through shared work;

Locally valid concepts of fairness and distribution.


Villagers lead tourists to carry out eco-tourism, introducing this place from a local perspective and knowing which route is the most beautiful while protecting the environment, 2015, Photo by the author



Digital Substitution as Emergent Protocol

Even as offline co-creation flourished, a parallel innovation emerged—digital participation via QQ group singing. Many local youth had migrated to cities for work, especially to factory towns along China’s eastern coast. Despite spatial separation, their emotional and cultural ties to home remained strong. In the absence of physical proximity, they began to reconstitute traditional duige (对歌) practices in virtual space.

Around 9 PM—mirroring the post-dinner social rhythm in villages—these youth would gather in QQ group chats and begin singing. Using the platform’s 60-second voice messaging function, one person would record a song stanza and send it to the group. Others were invited to respond, echoing the lyrical battle structure of face-to-face duige. Though constrained by platform limits (e.g., voice message time caps, asynchronous playback), participation remained spirited. Often, participants sent multiple consecutive voice messages to complete a single response, and these exchanges could last deep into the night.

One returned migrant, Yang Chengkui, reflected:

“When I was working in a factory, I missed home a lot. I missed the spicy food, the sour soup, and especially the evenings spent singing. After work, I’d have nothing to do, so I’d open QQ and start singing. It was fun—it made the night go by faster.”


Several group chats specifically for online duige (left) and duige in QQ groups (right)

This practice held unexpected advantages. Unlike live duige, which required all singers to be present in the same location at the same time, QQ-based singing allowed for asynchronous cultural presence. A participant could listen to the earlier exchanges hours later and still add a response. The interface inadvertently became a memory loop and interaction archive, enabling wider inclusion for those with fragmented time or weak connectivity.

In retrospect, QQ singing functioned as an early experimental protocol:

It had constraints (time caps, linear threading);

It offered affordances (asynchronous entry, persistent archive);

It fostered identity performance in a distributed setting.

Though not “designed” in the traditional sense, it showcased how emotional attachment plus minimal technology can co-produce structure. This insight later shaped the design of asynchronous roles and modular tasks in the Protocol Engine system.

This phase marked the emergence of distributed cultural infrastructure without the need for centralized platforms or institutional leadership. Through iterative engagement, both physical and digital, villagers and collaborators began enacting informal but effective protocols for participation, value sharing, and cultural transmission. Emotional ties, mutual visibility, and modular design principles allowed co-creation to flourish under conditions of economic uncertainty and geographic dispersion. The QQ duige practice, in particular, highlighted how minimal technologies could sustain affective and creative continuity across time and space—laying early groundwork for the Protocol Engine logic developed in subsequent phases.

4.3 Protocolized Co-Creation and Knowledge Systematization (2018–2019)

Design Challenge: How can distributed cultural co-creation be formalized through emotionally anchored, modular protocols?

This phase marked a turning point in the project’s evolution—from experimental collaboration to a protocol-based design system for distributed cultural production. Grounded in emotional attachment to heritage, and shaped through iterative co-design, the 2018–2019 initiative developed a fully structured cultural commons mechanism—without relying on commercial platforms or contractual governance.

The project was titled the Dong Song Picture Book Co-Creation, aimed at revitalizing endangered oral traditions among Dong children. However, the process also became a site of methodological innovation in participatory design, demonstrating how community-grounded, emotionally anchored protocols can guide large-scale distributed collaboration across boundaries of geography, class, and expertise.

Project Architecture: From Community Authority to Role Modularization

The ten-step system was collaboratively constructed between the researcher, village elders, youth leaders, and invited contributors. Although the researcher initiated the framework, critical authority rested with local actors, especially in decisions concerning song content, pedagogy, and interpretation.

1. Goal Definition
Through multiple meetings involving village heads, songmasters, youth leaders, and visiting educators, a shared mission was established: to make Dong songs accessible and meaningful to children aged 6–12 through multimodal creative formats (books, videos, performances, games). Song interpretation and cultural messaging would remain in local control.

2. Song Selection
All village songmasters were consulted in the selection process. Ultimately, the authority to choose fell to Master Yang Zhengli, a respected elder with deep knowledge of the repertoire. Six songs were selected based on difficulty, theme, and transmission value. Coordination and transcription were handled by returning youth leader Yang Chengkui, who translated lyrics and meanings into Mandarin.


The author (on the right) interviewed singers, village leaders to learn about their vision for the project, 2018, Photo by the author

3. Song Practice & Documentation
Yang Chengkui also led evening practices, coordinating children and documenting their learning with attendance lists, song progress logs, and video recordings. His logs formed a critical feedback loop in measuring engagement and performance growth.

4.6 Summary: Protocols as the Product of Design-as-Research

Over the course of a decade, the Dong Song Co-Creation initiative evolved from a localized educational effort into a distributed cultural commons infrastructure. Yet this transformation was not driven by platforms, policies, or contracts. Instead, it emerged through design.

The Protocol Engine developed in this project was not a digital tool or formalized governance framework—it was an ecosystem logic, shaped through iterative cycles of field engagement, modular role design, and emotional-cultural anchoring. What sustained collaboration was not structure alone, but the affective alignment of purpose, identity, and social meaning.

Unlike many models of open innovation that rely on incentives or institutional scaffolding, this framework scaled by subtraction, not addition: roles were designed to be easily replaced, tasks to be modular, commitments to be optional yet meaningful. The system allowed for exit without collapse, contribution without permanence, and coordination without hierarchy.

Each mechanism—whether emotional anchoring, cultural grounding, or permeable boundaries—was not imported from theory, but surfaced inductively through reflective design practice. In this way, the Protocol Engine itself became the central research output: not a singular solution, but a set of interdependent design propositions that enable distributed cultural co-creation under conditions of fragility, inequality, and absence of formal authority.

This chapter has laid the empirical and design-theoretical groundwork for interpreting how such a system functions—and what it suggests about design's role in commons governance. The following chapter applies three conceptual lenses—Common-Pool Resource Theory, Service Ecosystem Theory, and Interdependency Theory—to analyze how the Protocol Engine both aligns with and challenges dominant paradigms of coordination, sustainability, and legitimacy in collaborative systems.

Chapter 5: Theorizing the Protocol Engine – Toward a Framework for Distributed Cultural Commons

5.1 Introduction – From Practice to Theory

The previous chapter traced the emergence of the Protocol Engine across a decade of design engagement with Dong ethnic communities in Guizhou, China. What began as observation within a fragile learning environment gradually evolved into a distributed collaboration ecosystem—one sustained not by formal institutions or digital platforms, but by shared affect, trust, and cultural legitimacy. Through successive iterations, design became less a matter of producing outputs and more a matter of cultivating conditions: of belonging, reciprocity, and continuity.

This chapter turns from empirical narration to theoretical interpretation. It examines how the mechanisms that composed the Protocol Engine—such as role atomization, emotional anchoring, soft protocols, and community-led legitimacy—can be situated within, and also extend, three bodies of theory: Common-Pool Resource (CPR) theory, Service Ecosystem theory, and Interdependency theory. Each offers a different lens for understanding the same question that motivated this entire research: How can collaboration be designed and sustained when platforms, policies, or formal contracts are absent?

Design, in this sense, is treated not as the act of giving form, but as the structuring of interrelations. As Fry (2009) argued, design becomes ontological when it “shapes the conditions of existence rather than the things that exist.” The Protocol Engine emerged precisely as such a condition-setting mechanism—one that made possible distributed authorship without central control, and collective continuity without infrastructural permanence.

While digital networks and sharing platforms have often been framed as engines of distributed creativity (Manzini, 2015; Bruns, 2008), they typically rely on centralized ownership, algorithmic regulation, and asymmetrical value extraction. The Protocol Engine proposed here offers an alternative: a non-platform infrastructure that operates through soft governance, modular participation, and affective accountability. It demonstrates how communities can govern themselves through emotional reciprocity and cultural meaning rather than through formalized systems of enforcement.

To theorize such a system, this chapter proceeds in three interpretive movements.

Section 5.2 employs Common-Pool Resource Theory (Ostrom, 1990; 2010) to examine how governance emerged through social visibility and trust rather than rules and sanctions.

Section 5.3 reinterprets the project through Service Ecosystem Theory (Vargo & Lusch, 2008; Akaka et al., 2013), focusing on how value was co-created through distributed roles and cultural reciprocity.

Section 5.4 applies Interdependency Theory (Barad, 2007; Puig de la Bellacasa, 2017) to reveal how collaboration was not merely collective but relationally entangled—sustained through care and co-becoming.

The aim is not to fit practice into theory, but to allow practice to speak back to theory—to show how long-term, culturally embedded design engagements might extend or even reconfigure established models of commons governance and collaboration. In doing so, this chapter positions the Protocol Engine as a design-theoretical contribution: a framework for distributed cultural commons in which emotion, structure, and meaning operate as co-equal forces of sustainability.


5.2 Common-Pool Resource Theory – Reframing Governance

From Rule Enforcement to Emotional Reciprocity

Common-Pool Resource (CPR) Theory, most notably articulated by Elinor Ostrom (1990; 2010), challenged the long-held assumption that shared resources inevitably lead to the “tragedy of the commons.” Through empirical studies of irrigation systems, fisheries, and community forests, Ostrom identified a set of design principles that allow communities to self-govern without external authority. These include clearly defined boundaries, collective-choice arrangements, graduated sanctions, and monitoring mechanisms.

At first glance, the Dong Song Co-Creation project seems far removed from these examples. It dealt not with tangible resources like forests or fisheries, but with intangible ones—songs, rituals, and identities. There were no physical boundaries, no explicit rules, no monitoring officials. Yet the system persisted over a decade, involving more than two hundred participants and surviving both social mobility and the global pandemic. How, then, was governance achieved?

The answer lies in the substitution of affective reciprocity for rule enforcement. In place of explicit sanctions, the system relied on emotional anchoring—participants’ internalized sense of care, pride, and belonging. Commitment was sustained not through fear of penalty, but through the desire to “live up to” community expectations. As one songmaster put it, “This project has no contracts, but everyone shows up.” The absence of formal governance became a catalyst for trust-based participation.

This reconfiguration of governance challenges a core assumption in CPR theory: that effective commons management requires codified rules. The Protocol Engine demonstrates that emotion itself can serve as governance infrastructure—a non-institutional mechanism through which social order is maintained. The project’s mechanisms of reciprocal visibility (shared logs, public showings) and cultural adjudication (recognition by elders and peers) functioned as soft monitoring systems, ensuring accountability without coercion.

Soft Boundaries and Culturally Grounded Legitimacy

Ostrom’s first design principle emphasizes clearly defined boundaries—distinguishing who has rights to use the resource and who does not. In the Dong context, boundaries were not spatial or contractual, but cultural and emotional. Access to participation was guided by shared ethics—respect for elders, authenticity of representation, and willingness to learn. Newcomers, such as urban volunteers or artists, entered through a “mindset protocol” titled How to Enter as an Other, which replaced institutional onboarding with reflexive humility.

This boundary condition was permeable yet protective: open to diverse actors, yet anchored in Dong moral order. In this sense, the Protocol Engine replaces legal rights with relational rites. Cultural authority—embodied by songmasters and elders—served as the legitimate arbiter of decisions. Legitimacy thus emerged from cultural grounding rather than bureaucratic hierarchy.

The field observations show how this legitimacy functioned. When Master Yang corrected a child during practice, he did so not by imposing authority but through subtle affective cues—a smile, a gesture, a guiding tone. These micro-interactions enacted governance through care, embodying what Ostrom might call graduated sanctions in their softest possible form. Each gesture conveyed both expectation and acceptance, reinforcing communal values while preserving dignity.

Such affective modes of governance challenge the Western administrative bias embedded in CPR theory. The Dong system suggests that commons governance can operate through relational ethics rather than formal law, especially when the resource in question is cultural rather than material.

Visibility as Accountability

Monitoring, in Ostrom’s framework, is a critical function: individuals must observe others’ behaviors to ensure compliance. In digital or bureaucratic systems, this often takes the form of metrics, records, or surveillance. In the Protocol Engine, however, monitoring was achieved through social visibility—a design feature intentionally cultivated through participatory transparency.

For instance, during the 2014–2017 phase, a hand-written visitor ledger recorded which families hosted guests and what income was received. This simple artifact became a visible ledger of reciprocity: a form of social accounting that ensured fairness without central enforcement. Similarly, during the picture-book co-creation, volunteers publicly shared their progress logs and creative outputs, allowing others to observe, comment, and respond. This created a collective awareness akin to Ostrom’s mutual monitoring, yet grounded in emotion and empathy rather than authority.

Visibility also extended to rituals of recognition. The final exhibitions, song performances, and award ceremonies were not mere outcomes—they functioned as public audits of commitment. To be seen was to be accountable. The community’s memory served as the archive of governance.

In Ostromian terms, then, the Protocol Engine transforms monitoring from surveillance to celebration. It redefines compliance as care for continuity, where social recognition replaces punishment as the primary motivator.

Governance without Extraction

Ostrom’s later writings acknowledge that commons are not only about efficiency but also about equity (Ostrom, 2010). Yet in practice, many “commons-inspired” digital platforms—such as crowdsourcing systems—tend to extract value asymmetrically. The Dong case avoids this through what can be called non-extractive participation.

Because contributions were modular and voluntary, participants could enter and exit freely without destabilizing the system. Tasks such as song transcription, illustration, or documentation were self-scoped and emotionally meaningful. The value generated—picture books, videos, performances—was reinvested into the community, not privatized. This aligns with Ostrom’s emphasis on collective-choice arrangements, but extends it by integrating affect as the medium of choice.

Each participant negotiated their own balance between autonomy and obligation. The designer’s role was not to enforce participation, but to design conditions where contribution felt worthwhile. As a result, governance was experienced not as control but as care. The Protocol Engine thereby demonstrates how affective commons governance can emerge through design—transforming Ostrom’s institutional principles into experiential, relational ones.

Extending CPR Theory to Intangible Cultural Commons

By applying CPR theory to cultural co-creation, the Dong Song project extends the domain of commons research beyond material or digital resources. Cultural heritage is inherently relational: it exists not in possession but in practice. Managing such a resource demands not ownership but participation.

In this sense, the Protocol Engine embodies what scholars like Bollier and Helfrich (2019) call a “commons of meaning”—a shared field of value sustained through collective sense-making. Yet unlike many Western commons experiments that depend on formal cooperatives or online infrastructures, this project operated through embodied relationships, emotions, and oral transmission. It demonstrates that design can translate commons governance into cultural form, embedding it within everyday rituals rather than institutional frameworks.

In summary, through the CPR lens, the Protocol Engine can be read as a system of governance by emotion:

Boundaries were maintained through shared ethics.

Monitoring was enacted through visibility and celebration.

Sanctions were replaced by gestures of care.

Collective choice occurred through situated dialogue.

Legitimacy derived from cultural authority and mutual recognition.

These elements collectively reframed governance from a rational-instrumental process to a relational-affective one. The Protocol Engine thus extends Ostrom’s legacy into the domain of intangible, distributed, and emotionally sustained commons, where the core design question is not “Who enforces the rules?” but “How do we sustain care?”


5.3 Service Ecosystem Theory – Reframing Value Co-Creation

From Transaction to Reciprocity

Service Ecosystem Theory, grounded in the Service-Dominant Logic (SDL) articulated by Vargo and Lusch (2008, 2016), reconceptualizes value as something co-created through interactions among actors rather than delivered by producers to consumers. Within this framework, services are not discrete exchanges but ongoing processes of resource integration guided by shared institutions—social rules, norms, and meanings that coordinate participation across scales (Chandler & Vargo, 2011).

The Protocol Engine resonates deeply with this paradigm, yet it also departs from it in crucial ways. While SDL focuses on economic coordination within market or organizational contexts, the Dong Song Co-Creation project unfolded in a context largely devoid of markets or institutions. The “resources” being integrated—songs, time, stories, emotions—were non-commercial, embedded in social life. Value was not captured through economic exchange, but circulated through reciprocity, recognition, and shared belonging.

In this light, the Protocol Engine represents a commons-based service ecosystem, where each act of contribution—singing, illustrating, hosting, translating—constitutes a micro-service that sustains the whole. The ecosystem is not transactional but relational: participants do not “exchange” value but amplify it through interconnected acts of care. A child singing for a visitor, an elder lending jewelry for filming, a volunteer staying up late to edit footage—all participate in a distributed network of service that binds emotional, cultural, and creative capital.

This shifts the core of service design from efficiency to meaningfulness. As Kimbell (2011) suggests, service design is not about choreographing user touchpoints but constructing “social arrangements through which value is realized.” In the Dong project, those arrangements were built not by institutions but by protocols of care.

Role Atomization and Resource Integration

A defining feature of the Protocol Engine was its role atomization—the decomposition of work into modular, autonomous, and substitutable tasks. This structure allowed over a hundred contributors, dispersed across geography and expertise, to participate asynchronously without centralized coordination. Each role—illustrator, documentarian, translator, or musician—functioned as an independent node within the ecosystem, integrating specific resources (skills, time, tools) into a collective outcome.

In Service Ecosystem terms, this represents a distributed resource integration mechanism. Instead of a single organization orchestrating activities, the system relied on a shared purpose and minimal design scaffolds—guides, shared folders, and emotional norms. The value of each role was determined not by its economic worth but by its symbolic contribution to collective meaning.

Moreover, the modularity of roles allowed the system to absorb fluctuation. If one contributor withdrew, another could substitute seamlessly, ensuring what Manzini (2015) calls “design for fluid participation.” This fluidity created a resilient form of interdependence, where sustainability stemmed from redundancy and openness rather than control.

Emotional Value and Cultural Legitimacy

Traditional service models often assume rational actors motivated by utility. In contrast, participants in the Dong project were motivated by emotional resonance. Volunteers described their engagement as “meaningful,” “healing,” or “transformative,” emphasizing belonging over reward. This suggests an alternative form of value—affective value—that functions as a currency of participation in commons-based ecosystems.

Such emotional value was inseparable from cultural legitimacy. Contributions were only recognized as valuable if they respected Dong moral and aesthetic codes. A volunteer’s artwork, for example, had to align with local symbolism; a performance had to honor the song’s lineage. Legitimacy thus became a form of quality control that replaced managerial supervision. As Akaka and Chandler (2011) argue, value co-creation is guided by shared institutions; in this case, those institutions were embodied, oral, and emotional rather than formalized.

This combination of emotional value and cultural legitimacy allowed the ecosystem to self-regulate. Participants aligned their efforts with community expectations because they cared about the outcomes’ meaning. The designer’s role, therefore, was not to design services but to design the enabling conditions for affective reciprocity—a process that might be called service design for trust.

Asynchronous Coordination and Temporal Commons

Service ecosystems typically assume synchronous coordination—real-time co-presence or continuous feedback loops. Yet the Protocol Engine operated largely asynchronously. Tasks were completed across time zones, devices, and personal rhythms. A musician might transcribe a song weeks after fieldwork; an illustrator might deliver sketches months later.

This temporal dispersion did not weaken collaboration—it expanded it. The use of QQ, Trello, and cloud folders created a temporal commons, where contributions accumulated as shared resources accessible at any moment. Such asynchronous interaction transformed delay into depth: participants could reflect, adapt, and contribute when emotionally ready.

Theoretically, this extends Service Ecosystem thinking into the temporal dimension of commons. Value is no longer co-created in real-time encounters but across asynchronous, emotionally charged intervals. Time itself becomes a medium of reciprocity. This resonates with Ingold’s (2011) notion of “lines of becoming,” where creativity unfolds through the slow accretion of relational traces rather than instant exchange.

Reframing Value Co-Creation

Synthesizing these insights, the Protocol Engine reframes value co-creation in four key ways:


From Market to Commons: Value arises from shared meaning, not market exchange.



From Transaction to Reciprocity: Contribution is motivated by care, not compensation.



From Coordination to Emergence: Order emerges from shared affect, not managerial control.



From Efficiency to Relational Depth: Sustainability depends on emotional investment, not productivity.


In short, the Protocol Engine turns service design into cultural infrastructure design—an art of cultivating interdependence through shared meaning. It demonstrates how design can orchestrate participation without platforms, by aligning emotional and cultural logics within distributed ecosystems.


5.4 Interdependency Theory – Reframing Collaboration

From Individual Autonomy to Relational Entanglement

If the previous section situated the Protocol Engine within economic and organizational theories of value, this section turns toward relational and post-humanist perspectives to understand how collaboration itself is constituted. Interdependency Theory, as articulated by feminist scholars such as Karen Barad (2007), Donna Haraway (2016), and Maria Puig de la Bellacasa (2017), rejects the notion of independent agents cooperating through rational negotiation. Instead, it sees collaboration as co-constitution: entities become through relations.

In the Dong Song Co-Creation, this ontology was palpable. Participation was not an aggregation of individuals but an ecology of mutual becoming. The children learned by observing elders; volunteers learned by being corrected gently; the designer learned by listening. Knowledge flowed in loops of dependency rather than linear hierarchies. Every act of creation—drawing, singing, teaching—was both giving and receiving.

Barad’s (2007) concept of intra-action—where entities do not pre-exist their relations but emerge through them—captures this dynamic precisely. In the project, the designer was not an external facilitator but became part of the social fabric. As her role evolved from observer to weaver to enabler, her agency was continually reconfigured through others. Similarly, volunteers’ creative agency was shaped by the cultural authority of the villagers. Collaboration thus functioned as an entangled becoming, not a planned coordination.

Matters of Care and Emotional Labor

Puig de la Bellacasa (2017) extends this view through her notion of matters of care, arguing that ethical and political relations emerge through the maintenance of interdependence. Care, she notes, is both affective and material—it binds people while demanding attention to vulnerability.

The Dong project exemplified this. Every successful collaboration was underpinned by acts of care: mothers braiding children’s hair for filming, volunteers washing dishes after shared meals, elders lending silver ornaments for performances. These gestures, though peripheral to the “project tasks,” constituted the affective infrastructure that made co-creation possible.

Such care also operated at epistemic levels. Knowledge about songs, customs, and stories was shared through patience and humility. Volunteers were taught not only what to do but how to be—how to enter respectfully, how to accept correction, how to listen. These were protocols of care, designed not through documentation but through example.

In this sense, emotional labor was not supplementary—it was constitutive. The sustainability of the Protocol Engine depended less on technological scaffolding than on the continuous reproduction of care relations. Collaboration, therefore, was not the alignment of goals but the alignment of hearts.

Asymmetry and Mutual Transformation

Interdependency is never symmetrical. As feminist theorists emphasize, dependency relations involve difference, vulnerability, and uneven power (Tronto, 1993). In the Dong project, asymmetry was omnipresent: between urban volunteers and rural villagers, between designers and elders, between institutions and communities. Yet rather than suppressing these differences, the project designed ways to mediate them through rituals and protocols.

For example, the How to Enter as an Other training explicitly asked volunteers to acknowledge positional asymmetry. They were encouraged to listen more than speak, to participate through respect rather than leadership. This reflexive orientation transformed hierarchy into humility. As a result, power differences became generative rather than divisive—they created opportunities for learning and empathy.

This aligns with Barad’s (2012) argument that ethics arises not from erasing difference but from staying with the trouble of relational entanglement. In the Protocol Engine, design’s ethical task was precisely to hold the asymmetry open—to allow care and learning to flow across it without collapsing into domination.

Distributed Agency and Temporal Entanglement

Collaboration across distance introduces another layer of interdependency: the temporal. Participants who never met—illustrators, transcribers, editors—became co-authors through asynchronous contribution. Their agency was distributed not only across people but across time. A child’s recorded song might inspire an illustration months later; that illustration might, in turn, influence a teacher’s lesson plan.

This distributed agency exemplifies what Haraway (2016) calls sympoiesis—“making-with.” Rather than a designer orchestrating outcomes, the system evolved through collective responsiveness. No single participant held authorship; instead, authorship emerged from the ongoing recombination of contributions.

Temporal entanglement also fostered resilience. When the pandemic halted in-person activities, relationships persisted through digital contact and memory. Years later, when the community reconnected for the UNESCO award, the web of care reactivated instantly—demonstrating that the system’s strength lay not in structure but in affective continuity.

Reframing Collaboration

Through this lens, collaboration in the Protocol Engine can be characterized by five interdependent qualities:

Relational Ontology: Participants co-emerge through intra-action, not pre-existing autonomy.

Affective Infrastructure: Care and emotion are the material through which collaboration is maintained.

Reflexive Ethics: Asymmetry is acknowledged and negotiated, not denied.

Distributed Agency: Authorship and creativity are collectively enacted.

Temporal Durability: Bonds persist across absence through emotional memory.

These features position the Protocol Engine as a living example of what Escobar (2018) calls autonomous design—design that arises from within communities and affirms interdependence as a political and ontological principle.


Toward an Ethics of Entangled Design

Ultimately, Interdependency Theory reframes the role of the designer within the Protocol Engine. Rather than acting as a manager or innovator, the designer becomes a caretaker of relations—someone who designs with dependencies, not against them.

This repositioning carries significant epistemic implications. It suggests that design knowledge is not generated solely through problem-solving but through dwelling-with: through the slow, reflexive practice of maintaining conditions where others can act. In this way, the Protocol Engine becomes not only a model of collaboration but also a pedagogy of humility—teaching designers to engage the world as participants in ongoing, co-constituted processes of care.
